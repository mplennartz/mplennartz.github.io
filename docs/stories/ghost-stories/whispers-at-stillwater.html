📋 INITIAL REPORT

The Bureau received an urgent request from the Cason family regarding unusual phenomena occurring at Stillwater Estate, a historic property located in Fiddler's Green, West Virginia. The estate, originally constructed in 1693, has been in the Cason family for generations.

The case came to our attention through a personal connection - Investigator 89 received a direct appeal from family members reporting escalating supernatural disturbances. Given the investigator's intimate knowledge of the property and family history, this presented a unique opportunity for insider investigation while also requiring careful attention to potential emotional complications.

PROPERTY INFORMATION:
Name: Stillwater Estate
Location: Fiddler's Green, West Virginia (formerly Cross Hallow)
Construction Date: 1693
Original Builder: Elias Par (revivalist preacher)
Current Owners: William and Anne Cason
Historical Note: Built on site of 1700s revival baptism ground

The team arrived at the estate after a road trip through the misty backroads of West Virginia. The property looms dramatically over a glacial lake, its ancient timbers and flickering gas lamps creating an atmosphere of gothic foreboding. As autumn mists rise from the water, the estate's long corridors carry an unsettling silence.

📚 HISTORICAL RESEARCH

The property's documented history reveals multiple concerning elements that may contribute to current supernatural activity:

Original Site History: The estate was constructed on the location of a 1700s revival baptism site. Historical accounts describe disturbing incidents where individuals who were "drowned" during baptismal ceremonies and subsequently revived exhibited altered behavior - described in period documents as coming up "not right".

Elias Par, Original Builder: The revivalist preacher who commissioned the original structure had a controversial reputation. Contemporary accounts suggest he engaged in practices outside mainstream religious traditions, with specific reference to him "not always speaking in the language of God" - potential indication of glossolalia, channeling, or other spiritualist practices.

Name Change Mystery: The area was originally known as Cross Hallow, but the name was changed to Fiddler's Green around World War I. William Cason specifically mentioned that any historical documents referencing "Cross Hallow" should be preserved, suggesting awareness of suppressed local history.
