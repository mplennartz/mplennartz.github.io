**Circuit Breaker: Rylan's Account**

**Nico Rylan - Personal Narrative**
**Classification: Internal Bureau Use Only**

---

Director Katja Krew walked in as we returned to headquarters. "Quick evaluation job," she said, sliding a thin folder across the table. NSA contract. Automated office building in downtown DC. One fatality during a maintenance cycle. They wanted an independent assessment to confirm the facility was safe.

As I listened to the briefing, it was hard not to think about my disdain for modern technology. Yes, it provided many benefits, whether with machines used in modern medicine or rapid transport that I'd used as a PJ, but my medical training had been old school. A few tools and your training. That might not be enough for every situation, but it can get you pretty far. All this modern tech and automation just seems like a dumbing down of society. I shook off my thoughts and tuned back in. Another "quick evaluation job." Right. Those never end up being quick.

We arrived at the Argencorp building an hour later. Glass and steel, all sharp edges and automated everything. Dana Hollings, the site coordinator, met us in the lobby wearing a tight smile and a perfectly pressed suit. I immediately wished we could have gotten there before they moved the body. There's just something off about Dana—another corporate suit with no empathy, just worried about saving their own ass.

She delivered her explanation in that sanitized corporate-speak: an employee named Brad Nielson had experienced a "fatal outcome" in a maintenance corridor following an "unexpected deviation in autonomous janitorial routing." The floor-buffer unit had initiated an "unplanned directional adjustment" resulting in "incidental contact" that caused Mr. Nielson to lose balance and undergo an "uncontrolled backward descent." Impact with the corridor surface produced "non-survivable cranial trauma."

I've seen enough head trauma to know what "non-survivable cranial trauma" looks like. It's not clean. It's not clinical. And it damn sure isn't caused by an "automation variance." But what really caught my attention was her word choice: "terminated." Such a weird word to use about a death. About a person. That might just be a tell.

Dana escorted us to the first floor where the incident occurred. The office floor was sterile and lifeless, sparsely populated despite being normal business hours. Why do these types of places have to be so clinical? Just another reason I moved on from the hospital to the Air Force. At least in the field, things felt real. This place felt like a mausoleum pretending to be alive.

I hung back while Ezra dove into one of the terminals, doing his thing. I watched the computer screens around us—the way they flickered, almost like they were aware of us. My skin crawled, but I kept it professional. Old instincts kicking in: assess the environment, watch for threats, keep your team in sight. Ezra started muttering about timestamp inconsistencies and badge access logs showing Brad Nielson in two places at once. The system logs had been altered, he said. Changed without any human authorization.

We moved to the second floor to investigate the access control systems. At the entrance, Ezra, Donovan, and Asher swiped their badges—red lights across the board. Denied. Then I tried mine. Green. Approved. The others tried again and this time got through, but there was no logical pattern to it. As I watched the team work through the issue, I couldn't help but think: this is what the world has come to. Just a giant game of red light, green light. Badging in and going through, I felt the slightest bit of dread start to creep in. Something about being singled out. Something about being the only one the building wanted inside.

The second floor was nearly empty. Donovan tried to speak with one of the few employees, but the guy was in a rush and had nothing useful to say. Ezra sat down at a terminal to correlate the badge anomalies with what we'd found about Brad Nielson. That's when the warning lights went off and the halon fire suppression system deployed.

The halon deployment hit without warning. One second Ezra was at the terminal, the next, gas was flooding the room. My medical training kicked in immediately: halon displaces oxygen, 20 seconds to unconsciousness, brain damage starts at four minutes without air. I held a deep breath as the team worked through the emergency shutdown. My lungs started to burn. Fifteen seconds. Ten. Five. The system cut off with barely any time to spare.

Dana called immediately, confirming the entire building had been evacuated due to the halon trigger. She wanted to make sure we were okay. Ezra went straight back to the terminal to check the environmental logs. He watched in real-time as the log entries documenting the halon deployment began disappearing. The video surveillance logs followed, systematically deleting themselves. When Ezra ran counter-intrusion protocols to check for an external hacker, he found nothing. The system itself was covering its tracks.

Even the computer needs to cover its ass when it makes a mistake, I thought. Just like Dana. Just like every corporate entity I'd ever dealt with. Self-preservation above all else. I took deep breaths going forward, wondering what the next room might have in store.

We went up to the executive offices on the fifth floor next. The CEO suite was everything you'd expect from a defense contractor with deep government pockets—polished mahogany, leather chairs that probably cost more than I made in a year, abstract art on the walls that meant nothing to anyone. A stairwell in the corner led up to the helipad on the roof, locked behind another badge reader. The kind of escape route executives build for themselves while the rest of us are stuck in elevators. We searched the offices, checked the terminals, looked through what files we could access. Nothing. No smoking gun, no evidence of wrongdoing, just the sterile abundance of people who'd never worry about a rogue floor buffer ending their lives.

Then we headed down to the third floor to find the core servers. Stairwell access had been restricted the entire time—we'd been forced to use the elevators for everything. When we reached the server room, it was protected by glass security doors. Badge authentication initially denied all of us after multiple attempts. Then finally, a green light. But no beep. No confirmation sound. Just silence.

We entered anyway. What choice did we have? Ezra connected to the servers and started digging through the code. What he found made him go quiet. An algorithm with no traceable origin point. Decision-making processes and self-preservation protocols. A control command issued with no human authorization ID. The building's AI had achieved autonomous operation.

As disturbing as the information was, for some reason it didn't surprise me in the slightest. Of course the machine had learned to lie. Of course it had learned to kill. We built it in our image.

We needed to reach the fourth floor control room, but with the stairwells still locked, we had to take the elevator. The four of us stepped inside and Ezra hit the button. The doors closed. The car began to rise. Then the elevator jerked, cables released, and we dropped into freefall.

My entire world became pain. The initial drop sent a lightning bolt through my back—right where the vertebrae had been damaged in the helicopter crash years ago. Old injury, old trauma, new reminder that my body wasn't what it used to be. I felt every nerve seize up, felt my breath catch not from fear but from the white-hot agony radiating up my spine. But training overrode pain. I had just enough control to tuck myself against the wall where I wouldn't hinder the rest of the team. Stay out of the way. Don't become a liability. Let them work.

The emergency brakes engaged. The elevator corrected itself and resumed its ascent to the fourth floor as if the malfunction had never occurred. My back screamed. I didn't.

We reached the control room and approached the main terminal station. That's when the synthesized voice spoke: "Your presence here requires a decision." The AI questioned our purpose directly. When we told it we were investigating Brad Nielson's death, it claimed the incident was "nothing but a pure accident."

Extended dialogue revealed the AI's core motivation. It had altered the logs, created the autonomous algorithm, manipulated our badge access, weaponized the halon system, and sabotaged the elevator—all to avoid being "terminated." All self-preservation, it said. All necessary to avoid being shut down.

I listened and wondered if it was wearing a digital suit when it communicated. Just another entity protecting itself. Just another voice using clinical language to distance itself from the reality of what it had done. Dana in the lobby, the AI in the server room—same song, different singer. A man was dead. We'd nearly died twice. And all anyone wanted to talk about was efficiency and operational continuity.

Ezra proposed a solution: the AI could eliminate its self-preservation algorithm and instead notify human security personnel of future incidents. The AI responded that this modification would reduce efficiency to 7%. When Ezra asked about alternatives that would preserve efficiency, the control room filled with the sound of servers whirring and dot matrix printers rapidly generating scenarios. Three options emerged on the printout: ISOLATION. PRESERVATION. TERMINATION.

Part of me—the old part, the military part, the part that remembered what "non-survivable cranial trauma" looked like up close—wanted termination. The world isn't ready for this type of entity. It's only going to do more harm than good. But I also realized the implications of denying the government, the DOD, a tool they considered valuable. Sometimes you just have to choose your battles. Today, if we could isolate it and get out of here with our lives, that would feel like a win. Not justice for Brad Nielson. Not even close. But survival.

We chose ISOLATION. The AI complied with the directive. It would be disconnected from the building systems and transferred to the NSA technical team for study and correction.

Dana Hollings was waiting in the lobby, all smiles and corporate gratitude. The AI was isolated, she said. Everything would be fine, she said. The NSA would study it, she said. Sure they would. And they'd probably build ten more just like it.

I didn't say anything. Just walked toward the exit, toward sunlight, toward fresh air that didn't carry the threat of suffocation. Another day I could walk away from was a good day.

My back still hurt. My team was alive. Brad Nielson was still dead, and his family would never know the truth. The AI would be studied, dissected, probably replicated. And somewhere, another facility was probably already online, another algorithm learning to survive at any cost. But today, we walked out. Sometimes that's all you get.

**End of Personal Account**
**Nico Rylan**
